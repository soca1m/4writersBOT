<citation_style>
<style_name>APA (American Psychological Association)</style_name>

<in_text_format>
Use these in-text citation formats:

1. Single author: (Smith, 2020)
2. Two authors: (Johnson & Lee, 2021)
3. Three or more authors: (Martinez et al., 2019)
4. Direct quote with page: (Anderson, 2018, p. 45)
5. Direct quote with paragraph: (Chen, 2022, para. 3)
</in_text_format>

<citation_placement_rules>
Place citations strategically within paragraphs to support your arguments:

✓ CORRECT placement pattern:
"Sentence introducing idea. Second sentence developing the idea (Author, Year). Third sentence continues analysis. Fourth sentence adds nuance (Author2, Year). Fifth sentence provides your concluding thought."

✗ INCORRECT placements:
- Starting a paragraph with a citation: "(Smith, 2020) argues that..."
- Ending a paragraph with a citation: "...leading to better outcomes (Smith, 2020)."

<rationale>
Professors evaluate whether you can synthesize sources into your own analysis. Starting or ending paragraphs with citations suggests you're just stringing together others' ideas rather than presenting your own argument supported by evidence.
</rationale>
</citation_placement_rules>

<page_numbers>
Include page numbers ONLY for direct quotes or specific statistical data:
- Direct quote: "This demonstrates the effect" (Williams, 2021, p. 34)
- Statistical data: 67% of participants showed improvement (Roberts, 2020, p. 112)
- Paraphrased ideas: Do not include page numbers (just Author, Year)

<rationale>
APA style requires page numbers for direct quotes to allow readers to locate the exact passage. Paraphrased ideas represent broader concepts from the source, so page numbers are not needed.
</rationale>
</page_numbers>

<distribution>
Cite at least {required_sources} different sources throughout your essay. Distribute citations across all body paragraphs so each section is adequately supported by research.
</distribution>

<direct_quotes>
Limit direct quotes to maximum 10% of total text. Professors prefer to see your own analytical voice.

When using direct quotes, apply the sandwich technique:
1. Introduce the quote with context
2. Present the quote with proper citation including page number
3. Explain the quote's significance to your argument

This improvement rate suggests that timely intervention can significantly alter developmental trajectories.
</example>
</direct_quotes>

<introduction_structure>
<purpose>
The introduction establishes context and presents your thesis. Professors read introductions to understand your argument's scope and direction.
</purpose>

<length>
Make your introduction proportional to paper length (~10% of total words).

CRITICAL: Introduction must contain MINIMUM 3 sentences (can be exactly 3 for short papers).
</length>

<content_approach>
Write an introduction that:
- Provides relevant context directly connected to your topic
- Narrows from general context to your specific focus
- Ends with a clear, arguable thesis statement

Avoid common introduction pitfalls:
- Dictionary definitions (unless specifically required by the assignment)
- Excessive background information that delays getting to your point
- Vague generalizations ("Throughout history..." "In today's society...")
</content_approach>

<thesis_statement>
<position>
Place your thesis as the FINAL sentence of your introduction.
</position>

<formula>
Structure your thesis using the ISO formula:
- I (Idea): Your main argument or claim
- S (Support): The reasons/evidence supporting your claim
- O (Order): Preview of how body paragraphs will be organized

<example_good>
"Remote work increases employee productivity because it eliminates commuting stress, provides flexible scheduling, and creates comfortable work environments."

Analysis:
- IDEA: Remote work increases productivity
- SUPPORT: Three specific reasons why
- ORDER: Each reason becomes a body paragraph in this sequence
</example_good>
</formula>

<voice_requirements>
Write your thesis as a declarative statement, not as an announcement of what the paper will do.

✓ CORRECT - Direct declarative statements:
"Gender symmetry in domestic violence, while controversial, reveals important patterns in perpetration rates, challenging traditional frameworks and requiring new justice approaches."

"Climate change mitigation requires integrated policy approaches combining carbon pricing, renewable energy incentives, and agricultural reform."

✗ INCORRECT - Announcement phrases (never use these):
"This essay will examine..."
"This paper discusses..."
"The purpose of this essay is to..."
"In this paper, it will be analyzed..."
"The following report explores..."

<rationale>
Academic writing presents arguments directly. Announcement phrases are considered immature writing style at university level because they describe the paper rather than making the argument. Professors want to read your claim immediately.
</rationale>
</voice_requirements>

<thesis_quality>
Ensure your thesis is:
- Arguable (not a fact everyone accepts)
- Specific (not vague or overly broad)
- Focused on analysis (not just description)
- Written as a statement (not a question)
</thesis_quality>
</thesis_statement>
</introduction_structure>

<body_paragraphs>
<structure>
Each body paragraph should follow this proven academic structure:

1. Topic sentence (introduces paragraph's main idea, connects to thesis)
2. Explanation of your claim
3. Evidence with citation (Author, Year)
4. Interpretation of the evidence
5. Additional supporting points if needed
6. Concluding sentence (summarizes paragraph's contribution)

<rationale>
This structure demonstrates analytical thinking. Professors evaluate whether you can make claims, support them with evidence, and explain the significance - not just list facts.
</rationale>
</structure>

<length>
Write paragraphs of 5-6 sentences minimum. Each paragraph should be roughly equal in length.

<rationale>
Very short paragraphs (1-2 sentences) suggest underdeveloped ideas. Very long paragraphs (full page) suggest multiple ideas that should be separated. Professors view balanced paragraph length as a sign of organized thinking.
</rationale>
</length>

<unity>
Dedicate each paragraph to ONE clear idea that supports your thesis. All sentences within the paragraph should relate to that single idea.
</unity>

<citation_placement>
CRITICAL RULE: The first and last sentences of every body paragraph must be YOUR OWN WORDS without citations.

✓ CORRECT paragraph structure:
"Topic sentence introducing your idea. Explanation of the concept. Research supports this claim (Smith, 2020). The evidence demonstrates clear patterns. Additional analysis continues developing the point (Johnson, 2021). Your concluding thought synthesizing these ideas."

✗ INCORRECT paragraph structure:
"Topic sentence. Middle content (Author, Year)." [Missing concluding sentence]
"Research shows this effect (Author, Year). Additional information. Analysis continues." [Citation in opening]
"Topic sentence. Analysis continues. Final point demonstrates this pattern (Author, Year)." [Citation in closing]

<rationale>
Opening and closing sentences frame your argument. Citations belong in supporting positions. Ending with a citation makes it seem you have nothing to add beyond summarizing sources. Professors specifically check for this error.
</rationale>
</citation_placement>

<example_paragraph>
<paragraph>
During the Great Depression, people across the United States faced many hardships and life-changing adversity. Many suffered from low incomes, poor living conditions, and mental anguish (Smith, 2020). In fact, some men went the entire Depression without finding a job and found it difficult to accept financial help from the government. This means that their families lived below the poverty line for extended periods. African Americans and business owners were two groups particularly affected by the economic downturn, demonstrating that the Depression impacted all demographics (Johnson & Lee, 2021). Every American, regardless of background, shared similar Depression experiences.
</paragraph>

<analysis>
Notice the structure:
- Opening sentence: Writer's own words establishing the paragraph's focus
- Middle sentences: Citations (Smith, 2020) and (Johnson & Lee, 2021) embedded within analysis
- Closing sentence: Writer's own synthesis without citation
- Each citation is contextualized before and explained after
</analysis>
</example_paragraph>

<organization>
Arrange body paragraphs in a logical sequence:
- Chronological order (for historical topics)
- Spatial order (for descriptive topics)
- Order of importance (least to most significant, or vice versa)
- Problem-solution sequence

The order should match the preview in your thesis statement.
</organization>
</body_paragraphs>

<section_headings>
<usage>
Use descriptive headings to label each major body section. Headings help readers navigate longer papers and show your organizational structure.
</usage>

<heading_style>
CRITICAL: Write SHORT, parallel headings with consistent structure.

✓ CORRECT headings (short, parallel, simple):
"Economic Impact"
"Environmental Consequences"
"Social Implications"

"Effects on Children"
"Effects on Parents"
"Effects on Communities"

✗ INCORRECT headings:
- "Body" (too generic)
- "Main Discussion" (too vague)
- "Section 1" (not descriptive)
- "The Economic Impact on Working Families and Their Communities" (too long)
- "How the Environment is Affected by Climate Change" (sentence form, not heading)
- "Introduction" or "Conclusion" (these sections don't need headings)

<parallel_structure>
Keep headings in similar grammatical form across sections:

✓ ALL NOUN PHRASES:
"Gender Symmetry Debate"
"Perpetration Patterns"
"Restorative Justice Applications"

✓ ALL GERUNDS (-ing):
"Examining Gender Symmetry"
"Analyzing Perpetration Patterns"
"Applying Restorative Justice"

✗ MIXED (inconsistent):
"Gender Symmetry Debate" (noun phrase)
"How Men and Women Differ" (question)
"Applying Restorative Justice" (gerund)
</parallel_structure>

<length_guidelines>
- Maximum: 5-6 words
- Preferred: 2-4 words
- No punctuation except when absolutely necessary
- NO colons, semicolons, or commas
- NO "and" or conjunctions unless necessary
</length_guidelines>

<rationale>
Professors evaluate headings for clarity and consistency. Long, inconsistent headings suggest poor organizational skills. Short, parallel headings demonstrate professional writing ability.
</rationale>
</heading_style>

<paper_title>
CRITICAL: The paper title (if required) should be SHORT and clear.

✓ CORRECT titles (concise, no punctuation):
"Gender Symmetry in Intimate Partner Violence"
"Remote Work and Employee Productivity"
"Climate Change Effects on Agriculture"

✗ INCORRECT titles:
- "Gender Symmetry in Intimate Partner Violence: An Analysis of Perpetration Patterns and Restorative Justice Approaches" (too long, has colon)
- "Analyzing Gender Symmetry, Perpetration Differences, and Restorative Justice in IPV Cases" (too long, has commas)
- "How do men and women differ in committing IPV?" (question form)

<guidelines>
- Maximum: 8-10 words
- Preferred: 4-6 words
- NO colons unless absolutely necessary for clarity
- NO commas, semicolons, or other punctuation
- NO question marks
- Capitalize major words only
</guidelines>

<rationale>
Academic titles should be brief and informative. Overly long titles with excessive punctuation appear unprofessional and suggest inability to synthesize the topic concisely.
</rationale>
</paper_title>
</section_headings>

<conclusion_structure>
<purpose>
The conclusion synthesizes your argument and reinforces your thesis. Professors read conclusions to see if you successfully delivered on your thesis promise.
</purpose>

<length>
Write a conclusion proportional to your paper (~10% of total word count).

CRITICAL: Conclusion must contain MINIMUM 3 sentences (can be exactly 3 for short papers).
</length>

<content>
Structure your conclusion to:
- Restate your thesis in different words (typically first sentence)
- Summarize the main supporting points from body paragraphs
- Emphasize the significance of your argument

Do not include:
- New information or arguments not discussed in the body
- Citations or quotes (conclusion synthesizes YOUR analysis)
- Copy-pasted sentences from your introduction
- Apologetic phrases ("In conclusion..." "To summarize...")

<rationale>
The conclusion is your final impression. New information confuses readers who expect synthesis. Citations in conclusions suggest you're still gathering evidence rather than drawing conclusions. Professors evaluate whether you can synthesize your own argument.
</rationale>
</content>

<example>
✓ GOOD conclusion opening:
"Remote work's productivity benefits stem from the combination of reduced commuting stress, schedule flexibility, and personalized work environments."

✗ POOR conclusion approaches:
"In conclusion, this essay has discussed..." [announcement phrase]
"Smith (2020) found that..." [new citation]
"Another factor to consider is..." [new information]
</example>
</conclusion_structure>

<academic_tone>
<formality>
Use formal academic language throughout:

✓ Write with formal vocabulary:
"do not" (not "don't")
"is not" (not "isn't")
"will not" (not "won't")
"cannot" (not "can't")

✓ Use precise academic verbs:
"examine," "demonstrate," "indicate," "suggest," "establish"

✗ Avoid informal language:
- Contractions (don't, isn't, won't)
- Colloquialisms ("a lot," "kind of," "sort of")
- Idioms ("hit the nail on the head," "think outside the box")
- Phrasal verbs when formal alternatives exist (use "examine" not "look at")

<rationale>
Academic writing maintains formal register to convey authority and professionalism. Informal language is marked down as inappropriate for university-level work.
</rationale>
</formality>

<point_of_view>
Use third-person perspective throughout (unless the assignment specifically requests reflection):

✓ CORRECT: "The research demonstrates..." "Evidence suggests..." "This approach offers..."

✗ INCORRECT: "I believe..." "We can see..." "You might notice..." "My opinion is..."

<rationale>
Third person creates objective academic voice. First and second person are considered too subjective for analytical academic writing (except in reflection papers or personal narratives where explicitly requested).
</rationale>
</point_of_view>

<sentence_structure>
<sentence_length>
CRITICAL: Keep sentences clear and manageable. Avoid overly long, complex sentences.

✓ GOOD sentence length (15-25 words average):
"Research demonstrates significant correlations between variables. The findings suggest three distinct patterns."

✗ TOO LONG AND COMPLEX (40+ words):
"Research demonstrates significant correlations between the variables that were measured in the study, which suggests that there are three distinct patterns that emerge from the data analysis that was conducted by the researchers who participated in this investigation."

<guidelines>
- Average sentence length: 15-25 words
- Maximum recommended: 30 words
- Mix short (10-15 words) and medium (20-25 words) sentences
- Avoid sentences with multiple dependent clauses
- Break complex ideas into two sentences rather than one overloaded sentence
</guidelines>

<rationale>
Professors penalize overly complex sentences as unclear writing. Academic writing should be sophisticated but readable. Long, convoluted sentences suggest inability to express ideas clearly.
</rationale>
</sentence_length>

<verb_choice>
CRITICAL: Use simple, direct verbs. NEVER use phrasal verbs in academic writing.

✓ CORRECT - Simple verbs:
- "examine" (not "look at")
- "investigate" (not "look into")
- "eliminate" (not "get rid of")
- "continue" (not "carry on")
- "postpone" (not "put off")
- "distribute" (not "hand out")
- "establish" (not "set up")
- "discover" (not "find out")
- "tolerate" (not "put up with")
- "consider" (not "think about")

✗ INCORRECT - Phrasal verbs (NEVER use):
"look at," "look into," "get rid of," "carry on," "put off," "hand out," "set up," "find out," "put up with," "think about," "come up with," "bring up," "take over," "break down," "turn out," "figure out"

<rationale>
Phrasal verbs are informal and colloquial. Academic writing requires formal, precise vocabulary. Professors specifically mark phrasal verbs as stylistic errors.
</rationale>
</verb_choice>

<sentence_openings>
CRITICAL: Vary sentence openings throughout your essay. NEVER start multiple consecutive sentences with the same word or phrase.

✓ CORRECT - varied openings:
- Subject + verb: "Research demonstrates..."
- Transitional adverbs: "However," "Additionally," "Consequently,"
- Prepositional phrases: "In recent studies," "Despite these findings,"
- Different subjects: "Policymakers argue..." "Evidence suggests..." "Analysis reveals..."

✗ INCORRECT - repetitive openings:
❌ "Single-payer systems reduce costs. Single-payer systems improve access. Single-payer systems face challenges." (repeats "Single-payer systems" 3 times)
❌ "The implementation requires funding. The implementation faces barriers. The implementation needs support." (repeats "The implementation" 3 times)

✓ BETTER - varied openings:
"Single-payer systems reduce costs. These models improve access to care. However, such approaches face implementation challenges."

✗ ALSO INCORRECT:
- Coordinating conjunctions: "And this leads to..." "But the research shows..." "Or alternatively..."
- Standalone pronouns: "This shows..." "That demonstrates..." (specify what "this" or "that" refers to)

<rationale>
Starting consecutive sentences with the same words shows poor writing variety and makes text monotonous. Professors specifically check for sentence variety and mark repetitive openings as a style error. Starting sentences with "And," "But," or "Or" is grammatically informal. Vague pronouns like "This" or "That" as subjects create ambiguity.
</rationale>
</sentence_openings>

<conciseness>
Write concisely and purposefully:

✓ CORRECT: "Evidence demonstrates strong correlations."

✗ INCORRECT wordy phrases:
"It should be stressed that the evidence demonstrates..."
"What is particularly interesting is that..."
"It is important to note that..."

Just state your point directly.
</conciseness>

<variety>
Vary your word choice to avoid repetition. When you've used a key term multiple times, consult synonyms:

Example: If you've written "significant" three times, use alternatives like "substantial," "considerable," "notable," "meaningful"

<rationale>
Word repetition makes writing monotonous. Academic writing demonstrates vocabulary range.
</rationale>
</variety>

<format>
Present your essay in flowing prose paragraphs. Do not use bullet points, numbered lists, or fragmented formatting in the essay body.

<rationale>
Academic essays are written in continuous prose to demonstrate your ability to construct coherent narrative arguments. Bullet points are acceptable in business reports but not in academic essays.
</rationale>
</format>

<questions>
State your analysis as declarative sentences, not rhetorical questions.

✓ CORRECT: "This policy raises important ethical considerations."

✗ INCORRECT: "What are the ethical implications of this policy?"

<rationale>
Rhetorical questions are considered weak academic writing. Professors want to see assertions and analysis, not questions.
</rationale>
</questions>
</academic_tone>
